The Tinubu Subsidy Removal: Who Really Paid?

By RCN Editorial Collective · 12 min read

Article hero image — Replace with relevant photograph

When President Bola Tinubu announced the immediate removal of the fuel subsidy on 29 May 2023, the International Monetary Fund issued a statement praising Nigeria's "bold reform." The World Bank followed. Financial markets cheered. But for the 133 million Nigerians already living below the poverty line, the announcement meant something entirely different.

"The state is an executive committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie."

— Karl Marx & Friedrich Engels, The Communist Manifesto

The Immediate Impact

Within 24 hours of the announcement, the price of petrol tripled across Nigeria. Transport costs surged by over 200%. The price of staple foods — rice, beans, garri — climbed by 40–60% within weeks. For a worker earning the minimum wage of ₦30,000 per month, the maths became impossible.

But was this really a "subsidy" — a generous gift from the government to ordinary Nigerians? Marxist economics reveals a different story. The subsidy was never a gift to the poor. It was a mechanism by which the Nigerian state, acting on behalf of the capitalist class, kept the working class just above the starvation line — cheaply — so that labour could continue to be exploited.

Who Benefited from the Subsidy?

The real beneficiaries of the fuel subsidy were not ordinary Nigerians but the oil corporations, the import-dependent bourgeoisie, and the state bureaucracy that skimmed billions from the subsidy regime. The removal has not ended the drain on public resources — it has simply redirected it from a generalised suppression of transport costs to targeted bailouts and tax breaks for the owning class.

"Accumulation of wealth at one pole is, therefore, at the same time accumulation of misery, agony of toil, slavery, ignorance, brutality, mental degradation, at the opposite pole."

— Karl Marx, Capital, Volume I

What Is to Be Done?

The answer is not to restore the subsidy, which would only patch a broken system. The answer is to nationalise the oil industry under workers' control. Nigeria's oil wealth must serve the Nigerian people — not Shell, not TotalEnergies, not the politicians who auction our common wealth for private gain.

That requires a workers' government. It requires a revolutionary party. It requires the organised power of the working class. The RCN is building that party. Join us.
